Which language communities are most at risk in a crisis?

The Language & Crisis Risk Index is a single score that highlights the linguistic communities most likely to be cut off from information and help during a humanitarian emergency, because they have few speakers, little digital language support, and live where crises are active. A higher score means higher risk.

What the index measures

Every row is a language in a specific country, for example Dari in Afghanistan, or Chokwe in Zambia.

Each pair is scored from 0 to 1 by combining five groups of indicators (the index's pillars). Each pillar answers a different question about risk, and each carries a weight reflecting how much it counts toward the final score.

Where the data comes from

19 source files from established public datasets, refreshed at different cadences.

Every indicator traces back to a recognised provider: the UN, the World Bank, UNESCO, the ITU, and major open technology platforms.

How a score is built

Four plain steps turn raw numbers into one comparable score per language–country pair.

STEP 1
Put everything on one scale
Each raw measure is rescaled to a 0–1 range so speaker counts, dollars and percentages can be compared.
STEP 2
Flip the support measures
For tools, wealth and connectivity, "more" means "less risk", so those are inverted before combining.
STEP 3
Combine into 5 pillars
The measures in each pillar are pooled into a single 0–1 pillar score.
STEP 4
Weight & add up
The 5 pillars are blended by their weights into the final 0–1 risk score used for the ranking.

In other words: final score = (each pillar score × its weight), added together. Because the rescaling in Step 1 compares each language–country pair against all the others, the score is relative: it indicates which communities are most at risk within this set of 755 pairs, rather than an absolute probability.

The weights are not fixed in stone. The default weights (above) reflect the project's methodology, but the explorer can change them live (for example, raising Crisis Risk during an active emergency), and the ranking responds accordingly.

How reliable is it?

The index is a screening tool. A few things are important to read responsibly.

Missing data is treated as "no support"

When a figure is missing, it is filled in as zero before scoring. For support measures (most of the index) this is cautious: a language with no recorded translation tools is counted as having none. But for crisis measures it can under-state risk: a peaceful, high-income country that simply isn't tracked by crisis monitors will look safer than it is. Low crisis scores for small, well-off countries should therefore be treated with care.

Some pillars are shared across a country

Crisis, Comms & Tech, and Social & Economic data describe the country, so every language in that country shares those values. Only the Linguistic and Language Technology pillars vary language by language.

Known limitation: Language Technology. Most of the world's languages have almost no digital presence, so this pillar reads "high risk" for the large majority of communities. The scaling was improved so it now separates the better-resourced languages, but five of its seven measures only have data for fewer than ~140 of the 755 pairs. As a result the pillar still clusters near the top of the scale and is better at spotting the few well-resourced languages than at finely ranking the many under-resourced ones. Improving this is an active area of work.

The explorer has a built-in Data reliability panel that shows, for every measure, how much of the data is actually present, and in which regions, so the reliability of a given score can be judged.

The explorer tool

An interactive ranking of all 755 pairs, designed to be read at a glance and interrogated in depth.

Reading a row: the composition bar

Instead of a single number, each row shows one composition bar. The bar is split into coloured segments (one per pillar), and the length of each segment is that pillar's contribution to the score (its value × its weight). The segments add up to the row's total score, making it clear why a community ranks where it does, not only that it does.

Overview vs. indicator tabs
The overview ranks by the overall composite score. An indicator tab (e.g. Language Technology) ranks by that single indicator score and breaks its sub-measures down the same way.

Focus on selected countries
A multi-select picker pins any number of countries; the ranking narrows to their languages, and the selection stays in place while moving between the overview and every indicator tab.

Grouping by region or country
The list can be collapsed into continents or countries to compare whole groups, then expanded to the individual languages.
📋
Full detail on any row
Selecting a row opens a detail panel showing every raw value, how it was rescaled, and its contribution, with links back to the original source for each measure.
🩺
The data behind each score
The Data reliability panel and the per-indicator details card expose coverage, sources, update frequency and how missing data biases each measure.
🧰
Table tools (optional, collapsed by default)
A "Table tools" panel on the right edge offers advanced controls for users who want them: add or remove columns, search, change grouping and sorting, and view each column's distribution. It stays collapsed by default so the curated view stays uncluttered, and expands on click.

Where the tool could go next

Design directions under consideration. Feedback welcome.

A map view Show the ranking geographically, so funders can see where high-risk communities cluster and how risk varies across a region.
Saved priority profiles One-click weight presets for different perspectives ("crisis responder", "language-technology funder", "balanced"), so stakeholders can compare how each lens reorders the list.
Confidence on each score Pair every score with a visible reliability band, drawn from how much underlying data is actually present, so users know which rankings are solid and which are tentative.
Plain-language summaries An assistant that explains, in a sentence or two, why a given community ranks where it does and what would change its position, grounded in the real numbers.
Stronger Language Technology signal Refine how the language-technology pillar is built so it discriminates among under-resourced languages instead of grouping most of them together (see the limitation above).
